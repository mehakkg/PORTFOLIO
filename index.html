Point of view

The thesis.

Why most enterprise software fails — and what it takes to ship something people actually use.

In the age of AI, anyone can ship features.
Almost no one can ship enterprise software people actually adopt.

Enterprise users don't have the luxury of switching apps. They're locked in, overloaded, and deeply skeptical of anything new. The design challenge isn't to make something beautiful — it's to make something people trust enough to change how they work.

That requires understanding the system before touching the screen. It requires knowing which defaults to be opinionated about, which compliance constraints shape behaviour, and how to earn trust from an IT admin who has been burned by software promises before.

Core beliefs

How I think about the work.

Four things I'll defend even when it's inconvenient.

B · 01

Clarity beats cleverness.

Enterprise users don't have time to decode a clever UI. If the right action isn't obvious in 3 seconds, the design has failed — regardless of how good it looks.

B · 02

Systems before screens.

By the time I open Figma, the architecture is decided. Pixels are downstream of decisions. A poorly structured IA can't be fixed with better visuals.

B · 03

Adoption is the only KPI.

A product no one opens is a portfolio piece, not a product. Everything — onboarding, defaults, error states, empty states — is designed to reduce the cost of the first habit.

B · 04

Process is product.

How a design gets made shapes what it becomes. I document, I systemize, I write down what worked. The next designer should ship faster because I was here.
