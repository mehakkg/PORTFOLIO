Work / EducAItors

Designing trust into AI-graded academic submission: how a four-week sprint reframed a validation engine as a trust contract.

A grading module with an AI layer is not just a product problem. It is an emotional design problem. Students submit work not knowing if a machine or a human will judge them first. I redesigned the submission and feedback experience to make the AI feel like a collaborator, not a gatekeeper.

The thesis

When the system grades the student before the instructor does, every screen is a trust contract.

EducAItors built an AI module that reviews academic submissions before they reach a human instructor. The logic was sound: scale review capacity without scaling headcount. The experience wasn't. When a student submits an essay and the first response is algorithmic, trust collapses before it forms.

The original interface was built for the grading engine, not the person submitting. Upload state. Processing indicator. Score. No context, no reasoning, no acknowledgment that a human would ever see the work. Every screen communicated: the machine decided.

What students actually needed was not a faster grade. It was assurance that the process was fair, that their work was seen, and that disagreement had a path. None of those things were designed. They were assumed.

The brief: redesign the submission and feedback flow so the AI feels like a first-pass collaborator. Not a final judge.

"I submitted at 11pm and got a grade at 11:01. There is no way anyone read it. I do not know how to feel about that."

The problem

Academic submission is emotionally charged. Adding an AI gate raises the stakes on every screen.

Academic submission carries weight that most digital interactions don't. It's work that took weeks. It involves identity. When something goes wrong in a normal app, the user shrugs and tries again. When something goes wrong in a grading system, the user questions their ability.

The EducAItors module was designed the way most internal tools get designed: by engineers solving a logic problem. The AI model was well-tuned. The scoring rubric was solid. But the interface made the AI's reasoning invisible. Students received outputs without understanding inputs. Grades landed without context. Flags fired without explanation.

The result was a system that worked technically and failed emotionally. Resubmission rates were high. Educator inboxes were full of students challenging AI decisions, not because the AI was wrong, but because students had no basis to trust that it was right.

A trust deficit is a design failure, not a model failure. The model did not need fixing. The layer between the model and the student did.

"When the AI rejects something without telling me why, I assume it's wrong. A human would at least explain themselves."

Discovery

A persona-anchored audit. 9 personas, 4 functional groups, 33 design opportunities.

Before touching a wireframe, I ran a structured audit across every user type the product touched. Academic software has an unusual stakeholder map: the person using the interface, the person grading the output, the person managing compliance, and the person building the product are rarely in the same room and rarely have the same success metric.

I mapped 9 personas across 4 functional groups, walked each through the current submission and feedback flow, and tagged every friction point and moment of confusion. 80 raw issues collapsed into 33 named design opportunities after deduplication and clustering. One shared root: the AI's reasoning was invisible to everyone.

"I override the AI at least 3 times a week. Not because it's wrong. Because I can't see its reasoning and I can't justify it to my students."

ADOPTION RISK

From 80 opportunities to four rank reports.

Not all 80 issues were design problems. I grouped them by impact, frequency, and solvability and collapsed them into four structural failure categories that explained almost every reported problem.

Rank 01
Transparency failure

The AI's reasoning was invisible. Students received scores, not explanations. Every "why did I get this grade" support ticket was a transparency gap in disguise. 34 of 80 issues traced here.

Rank 02
Authority confusion

Nobody was sure who had final say: the AI, the instructor, or an appeal committee. Students submitted challenges against AI decisions not knowing if instructors had already reviewed them. 22 issues here.

Rank 03
Recovery gap

No clear path when students disagreed with an AI result. The only options were resubmit or email the instructor. No in-product challenge flow, no visible dispute route, no time estimate. 15 issues.

Rank 04
Emotional framing

Language was optimised for database outputs, not for students in high-stakes moments. "SUBMISSION REJECTED." "PLAGIARISM DETECTED." Both factually accurate. Both catastrophically framed. 9 issues here.

Phase 03 · Design

Three design decisions that reshaped the module.

Every screen change traced back to one of three structural decisions. Not feature additions. Architecture changes in how the AI communicated with the humans it was serving.

Decision 01
Feedback before grade. Always.

The AI output sequence was reversed. Feedback, dimension breakdown, and reasoning appear first. The grade appears after the student has read what it is based on. This single change reduced the "unfair" perception score more than any other intervention across both rounds of testing.

BEFORE
Grade: B+. Submission reviewed.
AFTER
Structure: strong. Argument: developing. Citations: good. Grade: B+.
Decision 02
The "Why?" layer, always one tap away.

Every AI-generated dimension score has a single-tap explainability panel. Not a tooltip. A full reasoning card that explains what the AI evaluated, what it found, and what would change the result. Available to students, educators, and compliance officers at different depth levels controlled by the user.

BEFORE
Argument depth: 62%. No explanation available.
AFTER
Argument depth: developing. [Why?] Central claim in §3 lacks a counter-argument. Add evidence for the opposing position.
Decision 03
Instructor presence, made visible.

The submission flow was redesigned to make the human review step explicit at every stage. The instructor's name appears before submission is confirmed. Review status is visible on the student's tracker. This one change reduced the "nobody is going to read this" anxiety pattern that was driving most resubmissions.

What didn't work, and how I adapted.

A four-week sprint with live students and a real academic product moves fast. Three decisions did not survive first contact with usability testing.

What I designed
What actually shipped / why it changed
Auto-grade reveal on submission: show the AI grade the moment submission completes. Designed for speed and transparency.
Removed. Testing showed immediate grade display increased anxiety before students had read the feedback. Grade now appears after the feedback breakdown, not before.
AI confidence percentage: show "AI confidence: 87%" alongside the grade to communicate model certainty to the student.
Removed. Students interpreted confidence percentages as a second grade, not a quality signal. Disputes increased, not decreased. Cut from the interface entirely.
Inline annotation mode: show AI feedback as margin annotations directly on the submitted document, like a teacher marking a paper.
Deferred to v2. Technical complexity exceeded the sprint window. Replaced with section-level feedback cards covering 90% of the educational value at a fraction of the build cost.
Bulk submission view for TAs: a multi-submission dashboard for teaching assistants managing 30-plus submissions per week.
Scoped to a queue view with status filters. Full bulk-action interface moved to v2. The queue covered TA needs adequately at current submission volume.

Designing inside reality.

Three constraints shaped every decision in this engagement. Working within them produced a better product than working around them would have.

Constraint 01
Regulatory uncertainty in AI grading

No established framework exists for AI grading in higher education. Every design decision had to be defensible to an academic board, a student welfare committee, and potentially an appeals tribunal. Transparency was not a nice-to-have, it was a legal requirement in disguise.

Constraint 02
High emotional stakes, low error tolerance

Students were in active assessment periods during the design sprint. No room for confusing interface states, ambiguous language, or feedback that could be misread as punitive. Every word choice in the UI went through a UX lens and a student welfare lens before it shipped.

Constraint 03
AI-literacy gap across the entire user base

Most students and many educators had no prior experience with AI-graded work. The interface had to explain AI decisions without patronising technically literate users or overwhelming anxious ones. The explainability depth was controlled by the user, not set by a default.
