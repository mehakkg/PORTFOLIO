The context

Access management is inherently complex. PAM sits at a fragile intersection.

Privileged Access Management is where security meets operations. IT admins use it to control who can access servers, databases, and infrastructure. Get it wrong and you get breaches. Get the UX wrong and admins bypass the system entirely — which is exactly as dangerous.

miniOrange's PAM product had shipped new capabilities every quarter for two years. The problem: capabilities were shipped, not designed. Every feature lived in its own drawer. Admins needed institutional knowledge just to find basic functions.

The consequence wasn't just user frustration. Enterprise customers were failing compliance audits because the audit trail UI was unusable. New customers were taking 4+ days to configure their first policy. Sales was losing enterprise deals in trials because buyers couldn't navigate the product themselves.

The brief was clear: fix the product, keep the engineering team, ship in 6 months.

Discovery

A scenario-driven UX audit. 4 personas, 4 scenarios, 140+ usability issues.

Before a single wireframe, I ran a structured audit: four IT admin personas across four core scenarios — onboarding a privileged account, responding to an access request, investigating a suspicious session, and running a compliance report.

Each scenario was walkthroughed with three enterprise customers and two internal sales engineers. I tagged every friction point, dead end, and confusing label. 140 issues. One shared root cause.

The product was built around database tables, not workflows. Every feature was a row in a table in a submenu in a tab. There was no concept of "what are you trying to do" — only "here is all the data, figure it out."

"I know it's in here somewhere. I just have to find it every time. I've been using this for 8 months."

From 140 issues to four scenario failures.

Not all 140 issues were equal. I grouped them by scenario and found four core failure patterns — each one blocking a different user goal entirely.

Failure 01

Onboarding: no guided path

Adding a new privileged account required navigating across 4 separate modules with no sequencing or dependency guidance. Admins routinely completed steps out of order and had to start over.

"Our customer success team spends 2 days with every new enterprise customer just to get them set up."
Failure 02

Access requests: wrong mental model

Just-in-time access requests were buried inside a "Requests" tab that most admins never opened. Emergency approvals during incidents required 7 clicks. Admins were sharing credentials in Slack instead.

"When a server goes down, I don't have time to find the approval screen. I just DM someone the password."
Failure 03

Audit trails: data but no insight

Session recordings existed but were stored in a flat table with no filtering, no anomaly surfacing, and no summary view. Compliance teams exported to Excel to do anything useful.

"I know the session recording is in there. Finding it takes 20 minutes. That's why we're failing audits."
Failure 04

Policy configuration: no defaults

Every single configuration field required a decision. Password rotation frequency, session timeout, MFA enforcement — all blank, all requiring expert-level decisions from day one.

"I don't know what the right values are. Just tell me what most companies your size use."

The work

The process that worked.

Six months, five phases. Each one built on the last, with output from one phase serving as direct input for the next.

Phase 01
Scenario audit

4 personas × 4 scenarios × real customer sessions. 140+ issues tagged and prioritised by severity and frequency.

Phase 02
Information architecture

Rebuilt the nav structure around user intent, not database tables. Three primary areas: Accounts, Access, and Audit.

Phase 03
Deal-breaker fixes

Four flows that were losing enterprise deals in trials. Fixed first, shipped first, measured before building anything else.

Phase 04
Experience prioritisation

From feature-led to experience-led — designed around the scenario, not the capability. Every screen earns its place.

Phase 05
Design system

Documented the component library, token set, and usage rules. The next designer can ship 3× faster because this exists.

Phase 03

Identifying the deal-breakers.

Not all 140 issues needed solving to unlock enterprise conversions. Four scenarios were responsible for 80% of trial drop-offs. I fixed those first.

Deal-breaker 01
Onboarding wizard — 23 steps → 4

Rebuilt the account onboarding flow around the most common enterprise configuration. Sensible defaults pre-filled throughout. Advanced options collapsed behind a "Customise" toggle. New accounts reach first working policy in under 30 minutes.

Deal-breaker 02
JIT access tray — always visible, always one action

Pending access requests now live in a persistent tray that's visible on every screen. Approve or deny in one click with inline context — no navigation required. Emergency approvals in under 10 seconds.

Deal-breaker 03
Session audit — filterable timeline, not a flat table

Replaced the raw session log with a filterable timeline. Sessions grouped by account, date, and risk signal. Anomaly flags surface automatically. Compliance export preserved — but now it's a last resort, not the only option.

Deal-breaker 04
Policy defaults — opinionated presets by industry

Added three pre-configured policy templates: Standard Enterprise, Financial Services (SOC 2), and Healthcare (HIPAA). Admins choose a baseline and override only what they need to. Configuration burden reduced by ~60%.

Phase 04

Experience prioritisation. From 'feature-led' to 'experience-led'.

A feature-led product adds capabilities. An experience-led product asks: what is the user trying to do, and what's the fastest path from here to there? Every screen in the redesigned PAM answers one question, not four.

The redesigned dashboard shows: accounts needing attention, pending access requests, and recent anomalies — in that order. Not a firehose of data. A prioritised action queue.

The redesigned session audit shows: a timeline of what happened, who was involved, and what the risk signals were. One click to expand. One click to export. No context switching required.

The redesigned policy editor shows: what's currently set, what the industry baseline is, and what's different — so admins understand their posture without reading documentation.

These design decisions that reshaped the product.

Decision 01
Intent-first navigation

Replaced the database-mirroring nav with three intent areas: Manage (accounts and policies), Respond (access requests), and Review (audit and sessions). Users know where to go because they know what they're doing.

Decision 02
Persistent action tray

Pending access requests live in a corner tray visible from every screen, so emergencies don't require navigation. Dismissed automatically when resolved. Configurable urgency thresholds per team.

Decision 03
Risk-first account list

Default sort on the account list is now by risk signal, not alphabetical. Accounts needing attention float to the top. Admins don't need to know what to look for — the system surfaces it.

Decision 04
Progressive policy editor

Policy configuration now shows: the current value, the industry baseline, and the delta. Advanced fields collapsed behind "Customise". Admins understand their security posture without needing documentation.

Decision 05
Contextual error recovery

Every failure state — failed login, expired session, misconfigured policy — now includes what went wrong, why it likely happened, and the exact steps to fix it. No raw error codes. No dead ends.

Decision 06
Design system as product

Shipped a component library alongside the redesign: tokens, patterns, and usage rules documented in Figma with engineering annotations. Future features can be built without reinventing layout or interaction logic.

What didn't work — and how I adapted.

A 6-month engagement with two engineers moves fast. Not every decision survived contact with engineering reality or customer feedback.

Designing inside reality.

Enterprise security products don't exist in a vacuum. Three constraints shaped every decision.

Constraint 01
Compliance first, aesthetics second

Every audit trail feature had to meet SOC 2 and ISO 27001 requirements before it could be "designed well". Working with the compliance team early meant no surprises at handoff.

Constraint 02
Enterprise hardware runs slow

Key customers run PAM on on-premise servers from 2017. Animation-heavy UIs lag, large data loads time out. The design had to perform at 2GB RAM and 30fps before it could be called done.

Constraint 03
No design debt budget

Two engineers, six months. No time for a full rewrite. Every change had to layer on top of the existing front-end framework without requiring architectural changes below the design layer.
