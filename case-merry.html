The problem

When seconds matter, fragmentation becomes dangerous.

Merry Health's emergency coordination team was running three separate tools to manage a single dispatch: a legacy CAD system for incident logging, a spreadsheet for unit availability, and WhatsApp + phone calls for hospital capacity. None of them talked to each other.

During peak load, coordinators were switching tabs, checking rows, and managing phone calls simultaneously while handling multiple active incidents. Decisions were delayed. Vehicles were misrouted. And the system designed to save lives was adding minutes to every response.

The error wasn't human. It was architectural. Every tool was built for data entry, not decision-making. Information existed in three places, in three formats, at three different moments in time. No one had ever designed the layer between the data and the people who needed to act on it.

The brief: one system, one screen, built around how coordinators actually think under pressure — not how the database was structured.

The context

The system already existed. It just wasn't designed.

Merry Health wasn't starting from zero. They had incident logs, unit tracking, and hospital capacity data. The problem wasn't missing data — it was that no one had ever designed the layer between the data and the coordinators who needed to act on it.

Each tool was built by a different team for a different purpose. The CAD for logging. The spreadsheet as a coordinator workaround. The phone lines because neither answered "which hospital has a bed right now." Three tools, zero coordination.

The design challenge was not technical. It was: figure out what coordinators actually need to see, when they need to see it, and what action they need to take — under operational pressure, with no time to think. Nobody had attempted this. There was zero prior UX research on coordinator mental models in this context.

"We don't need a better dashboard. We need to stop switching between three apps during a cardiac call."

Four insights reshaped the product direction.

After four coordinator shifts and eight debrief interviews, four findings changed the design direction entirely — and simplified the solution considerably.

Insight 01
Admins needed operational clarity, not more dashboards.

What looks like a tooling problem on the surface is a clarity problem underneath. We didn't need richer charts — we needed a single screen that answered "what's happening right now, and who's doing what?"

Insight 02
Behavioral adoption mattered more than technical sophistication.

Drivers were on feature phones. Admins were on WhatsApp. A "better" product that ignored where people already worked would be route-around'd on day one. We had to meet behavior where it lived.

Insight 03
Drivers required low-complexity interaction models.

One action per screen. Big tap targets. No multi-step decision trees mid-emergency. The driver's job is to drive — the product had to keep up with them, not the other way around.

Insight 04
Trust had to exist before automation.

No one would let an algorithm dispatch in their place until they trusted the system to be operationally accurate. Trust comes from consistency and visibility — not from feature labels.

Adaptive before automation.

The design principle that shaped every decision: the system adapts to the coordinator. Automation comes only after the adaptive layer earns trust.

Automation-first thinking

The design

One operational source of truth.

The redesigned system collapses three tools into a single, always-current view. No tabs. No cross-referencing. No phone calls to confirm what the screen should already know.

Designed for moments of loss.

Three features designed specifically for the hardest moments — mass casualty events, shift handoffs, and hospital capacity failures.

Feature 01

Surge mode

One-click mode that strips the interface to essential controls. Hides non-critical notifications, collapses secondary panels, enlarges incident cards — designed for 8–12 incident load at MCI scale. Toggled by a single persistent button, never buried.

"I can't read anything during a surge. I need bigger, simpler, fewer."
Feature 02

Structured shift handoff

At shift change, the system generates a live summary: active incidents, units in transit, pending decisions, unconfirmed hospital beds. Incoming coordinator reviews and accepts — no dropped incidents, no verbal briefing from memory.

"Handoffs are where things get lost. There's no standard — it's whoever has time to brief."
Feature 03

Hospital capacity layer

Live hospital ED capacity surfaced directly on the dispatch recommendation card. If the nearest hospital is full for the incident type, the system routes to the next available — no phone calls to confirm.

"The worst outcome isn't a slow dispatch. It's arriving at a hospital that can't take the patient."
Feature 04

One-tap dispatch with undo

Confirming a dispatch is a single tap with a 5-second undo window. Removed every confirmation dialog from the critical path. For a decision made 40+ times per shift, friction compounds into meaningful delay.

"Every extra click during a surge is a second I can't get back."

Constraints shaped the product.

Three constraints that most designers would try to work around became the product's core strengths instead.

Constraint 01

Aging hardware

The dispatch centre runs on machines from 2016. No animation-heavy UIs. No live map rerendering. The constraint forced the design into a layout that's genuinely faster to parse — because it has no visual noise.

"If it looks fancy, it probably won't run. We've been burned by that before."
Constraint 02

No time for training

Coordinators can't take training days. The system had to be learnable in one shift. This forced every interaction to be self-evident — labelling and layout carry all the weight with no tooltips or onboarding flows.

"We don't have time to train people. They figure it out on the job or they don't."
Constraint 03

Experts, not beginners

The users are domain experts. They don't need the system to explain emergency medicine. They need it to remove friction between their expertise and the outcome. Every "helpful" affordance that slowed an expert was cut.

"I've done this for 11 years. Don't make me click through a tutorial."
Constraint 04

Four weeks, not four months

A 4-week timeline forced ruthless prioritisation. One scoring rubric: impact on decision speed × frequency of use × feasibility. Anything not in the top 40% was explicitly deferred with a reason on record.

Scope discipline is a design skill. Most of what got deferred was correct prioritisation, not failure.

What this project taught me.

Three things I now apply on every high-stakes operational design problem.

Lesson 01
Research in the environment, not about it

Reading about emergency coordination and observing it are two entirely different inputs. The four shifts I spent embedded changed almost every assumption I brought in. No desk research would have surfaced "coordinators don't read during surge."

Lesson 02
Transparent systems earn trust; opaque ones get ignored

Every time the design surfaced a recommendation without showing its reasoning, coordinators said they'd override it. Make the reasoning visible, and experienced professionals will start relying on the system.

Lesson 03
Constraints are design material, not obstacles

The aging hardware constraint I initially tried to work around became the reason the final interface was faster to parse than anything I'd have built without it. The no-training constraint produced more intuitive labelling than any usability test alone.
