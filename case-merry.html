The problem

When seconds matter, fragmentation becomes dangerous.

Merry Health's emergency coordination team was running three separate tools to manage a single dispatch: a legacy CAD for incident logging, a spreadsheet for unit availability, and WhatsApp plus phone calls for hospital capacity. None of them talked to each other.

During peak load, coordinators switched tabs, checked rows, and managed phone calls simultaneously while handling multiple active incidents. Decisions got delayed. Vehicles got misrouted. And the system designed to save lives was adding minutes to every response.

The error was not human. It was architectural. Every tool was built for data entry, not decision-making. Information existed in three places, three formats, at three different moments in time. Nobody had designed the layer between the data and the people who needed to act on it.

The brief: one system, one screen, built around how coordinators actually think under pressure, not how the database was structured.

The context

The system already existed. It just wasn't designed.

Merry Health had incident logs, unit tracking, and hospital capacity data. The problem was not missing data. It was that no one had designed the layer between the data and the coordinators who needed to act on it.

Each tool was built by a different team for a different purpose. The CAD for logging. The spreadsheet as a coordinator workaround. The phone lines because neither answered which hospital had a bed right now. Three tools, zero coordination.

The design challenge was not technical. It was: figure out what coordinators actually need to see, when they need to see it, and what action they need to take, all under operational pressure with no time to think. Nobody had attempted this. There was zero prior UX research on coordinator mental models in this context.

"We don't need a better dashboard. We need to stop switching between three apps during a cardiac call."

§ 05 · Research process

Understanding pressure before designing screens.

Five steps. Two weeks of research. Two weeks of design. No discovery-then-throw-over-wall. Every step fed the next.

STEP 01
Data audit

Identified the minimum operational data required to dispatch under pressure, without paralysis from a hundred fields.

STEP 02
User journey mapping

Mapped patient anxiety, admin overload, and driver communication gaps as one continuous story.

STEP 03
Product audit

Located the fragmented workflows, the broken visibility, and the operational trust deficit the existing tools couldn't address.

STEP 04
Scenario mapping

Built workflows around emergency dispatch, assignment, live tracking, delayed response, and confirmation loops.

STEP 05
Prioritization

Locked focus on the highest-impact operational bottlenecks. Resisted the urge to expand surface area.

Four insights reshaped the product direction.

After four coordinator shifts and eight debrief interviews, four findings changed everything and simplified the solution considerably.

Insight 01
Admins needed operational clarity, not more dashboards.

What looks like a tooling problem on the surface is a clarity problem underneath. We didn't need richer charts. We needed a single screen that answered "what's happening right now, and who's doing what?"

Insight 02
Behavioral adoption mattered more than technical sophistication.

Drivers were on feature phones. Admins were on WhatsApp. A "better" product that ignored where people already worked would be route-around'd on day one. We had to meet behavior where it lived.

Insight 03
Drivers required low-complexity interaction models.

One action per screen. Big tap targets. No multi-step decision trees mid-emergency. The driver's job is to drive. The product had to keep up with them, not the other way around.

Insight 04
Trust had to exist before automation.

No one would let an algorithm dispatch in their place until they trusted the system to be operationally accurate. Trust comes from consistency and visibility, not from feature labels.

Design principles

Five principles every decision ran through.

Behavior

Design around what people already do, not what they should do. Admins were on WhatsApp. Drivers were on cheap Androids. The product had to integrate with existing behavior, not replace it.

Trust

Every recommendation had to be explainable. Coordinators only rely on a system they understand. Every suggestion shows its reasoning. Nothing happens without human confirmation.

Centralization

Three tools became one screen. Every action a coordinator takes, every status update a driver sends, every bed count from a hospital: one place, one truth, no switching.

Adoption

A system nobody uses does nothing. We made switching costs low, learning time under one shift, and immediate value obvious. Adoption was a design constraint, not an afterthought.

Automation

Automation earns its place after trust is established. V1 suggests, never decides. Recommendations with visible reasoning build the trust that makes automation safe to introduce in V2.

Adaptive before automation.

The system adapts to the coordinator. Automation comes only after the adaptive layer earns trust.

§ 08 · Solution architecture

One operational source of truth.

Three solution layers, built to layer on top of existing behavior, not replace it.

Solution 01
WhatsApp-assisted coordination

Preserve the existing behavior. Capture information through the channel admins already use, then route it into the system automatically.

Solution 02
Centralized operational dashboard

A single coordination layer. Real-time visibility, audit-friendly workflows, and incident processing in one place.

Solution 03
Dashboard + WhatsApp integration

An alternate coordination flow that fuses booking and tracking through the channel admins trust. Reduced communication delays.

Designed for moments of stress.

Every design decision was tested against the worst-case scenario: surge mode, mass casualty, shift changeover with active incidents. If a feature didn't hold up under stress, it didn't ship.

Stress test 01
Surge mode

One-click mode strips the interface to essential controls. Non-critical notifications hide, secondary panels collapse, incident cards enlarge. Designed for 8 to 12 incident load at MCI scale.

Stress test 02
Zero reading time

Coordinators process by colour and position during surge, not by text. Every status is a colour. Every priority is a badge. The screen communicates in under a second without requiring a single word to be read.

Stress test 03
Shift handoff under load

Structured handoff mode generates a live summary at shift change: active incidents, units in transit, pending decisions, unconfirmed beds. Incoming coordinator reviews and accepts. No verbal briefing from memory.

Stress test 04
Hospital capacity failure

If the nearest hospital is at capacity for the incident type, the system reroutes to the next available before dispatch is confirmed. No phone calls. No mid-dispatch discovery.

Stress test 05
Feature phone constraints

Driver companion runs as a PWA on sub-$80 Android devices. No animations, no complex layouts, no gestures. One button per state. Works at 2G speeds with occasional connectivity drops.

Stress test 06
One-tap undo window

Dispatch is a single tap with a 5-second undo window. No confirmation dialogs on the critical path. For a decision made 40 times per shift, removing one click saves minutes across the day.

Constraints shaped the product.

Three constraints that most designers would try to work around became the product's core strengths instead.

Constraint 01

Aging hardware

The dispatch centre runs on machines from 2016. No animation-heavy UIs, no live map rerendering. The constraint forced the design into a layout that's genuinely faster to parse because it has no visual noise.

"If it looks fancy, it probably won't run. We've been burned by that before."
Constraint 02

No time for training

Coordinators can't take training days. The system had to be learnable in one shift. This forced every interaction to be self-evident. Labelling and layout carry all the weight with no tooltips or onboarding flows.

"We don't have time to train people. They figure it out on the job or they don't."
Constraint 03

Experts, not beginners

The users are domain experts. They don't need the system to explain emergency medicine. They need it to remove friction between their expertise and the outcome. Every "helpful" affordance that slowed an expert got cut.

"I've done this for 11 years. Don't make me click through a tutorial."
Constraint 04

Four weeks, not four months

A 4-week timeline forced ruthless prioritisation. One scoring rubric: impact on decision speed × frequency of use × feasibility. Anything not in the top 40% was explicitly deferred with a reason on record.

Scope discipline is a design skill. Most of what got deferred was correct prioritisation, not failure.

What this project taught me.

Three things I now apply on every high-stakes operational design problem.

Lesson 01
Research in the environment, not about it

Reading about emergency coordination and observing it are two entirely different inputs. The four shifts I spent embedded changed almost every assumption I brought in. No desk research would have surfaced that coordinators don't read during surge.

Lesson 02
Transparent systems earn trust. Opaque ones get ignored.

Every time the design surfaced a recommendation without showing its reasoning, coordinators said they'd override it. Make the reasoning visible and experienced professionals will start relying on the system.

Lesson 03
Constraints are design material, not obstacles

The aging hardware constraint I initially tried to work around became the reason the final interface was faster to parse than anything I'd have built without it. The no-training constraint produced more intuitive labelling than any usability test alone.
